The Mind's Glitches: Cognitive Biases

Flowchart of a Bias: The Confirmation Bias Loop

Cognitive biases are mental shortcuts that can lead to errors in judgment. Confirmation bias, one of the most powerful, is our tendency to favor information that confirms our existing beliefs. This is how it can trap us in an echo chamber.

🤔

Pre-existing Belief

"I'm not good at public speaking."

🔍

Seek Confirming Evidence

Notice every stumble, ignore fluent moments.

🙈

Ignore Contradicting Evidence

Dismiss compliments; forget successful presentations.

🔒

Belief Strengthened

"See? I really am bad at this."

✨ Breaking the Loop:

Actively seek disconfirming evidence, challenge your assumptions, and consider alternative perspectives.

The Dark Arts: Recognizing Manipulation

Understanding manipulation tactics is not about paranoia; it's about **empowerment**. It gives you the tools to protect your mental space and maintain healthy boundaries. Gaslighting is a particularly insidious form where someone makes you question your own reality.

1. Countering

They question your memory of events, even when you're sure. "That never happened. You're imagining things."

2. Discounting

They minimize your feelings to make you feel like your reaction is invalid. "You're being too sensitive. It's not a big deal."

3. Denial & Diversion

They flatly deny saying or doing something, then change the subject to blame you. "I never said that! Why are you always attacking me?"

4. Questioning Sanity

A direct attack on your mental state. "You sound crazy right now. Everyone thinks you're unstable."

5. Love-Bombing

Overwhelming you with affection and praise to create dependency, which is later used for control. "You're amazing! I've never met anyone like you... but you're being so difficult lately."

6. DARVO

A common response to being held accountable: **D**eny, **A**ttack, and **R**everse **V**ictim and **O**ffender. "I didn't do it, and you're the one who's manipulative!"

The Timeline of Psychology

1879

Wilhelm Wundt

Establishes the first psychology lab in Leipzig, Germany, marking the formal beginning of psychology as a science.

1900

Sigmund Freud

Publishes "The Interpretation of Dreams," introducing the foundational concepts of psychoanalysis, including the unconscious mind.

1913

John B. Watson

Formalizes the school of behaviorism with his paper "Psychology as the Behaviorist Views It."

1950s

Carl Rogers

Pioneers humanistic psychology, emphasizing self-actualization and unconditional positive regard.

1960s

George A. Miller

The "cognitive revolution" shifts focus back to mental processes, a rejection of the purely behavioral approach.
